# Art Intelligence
*Beowolff · Zachary F. Mainen, Champalimaud Research*

**The problem.** Artsy has three million user profiles and twenty years of behavioral data. The recommendation engine that sits on top of that data is a collaborative filter — it surfaces what people like you have bought. This works for books and films. It fails for art, for a reason that turns out to be scientifically interesting: art preference splits into two components that do not reduce to each other. The first is visual — colour, line, composition, texture, scale. The second is meaning — what the work refers to, the tradition it inhabits, the artist's position in the market's narrative. A Hiroshi Sugimoto seascape and a Mark Rothko colour field may be statistically indistinguishable to a collaborative filter. They are not interchangeable to the collector who owns one.

No recommendation system has successfully modelled both components together. This is not an engineering failure — it is a scientific problem, and it has a scientific answer.

**The science.** Over the past decade, a convergence between computational neuroscience and visual AI has produced quantitative models of aesthetic preference. Work from our group, led by Kyo Iigaya (Iigaya et al., *Science Advances* 2021; *Nature Communications* 2023) shows that 45% of variance in visual preference across a large population is explained by a three-component model: low-level visual statistics (handled well by the first layers of convolutional networks), mid-level perceptual structure, and high-level semantic content. Critically, the model identifies three distinct taste clusters in the population — profiles that are stable, predictive, and not reducible to demographics or purchase history.

This is the scientific foundation. What it enables, practically, is a recommendation that tells a collector not just what they have bought but what they find beautiful — and why.

**The team.** Zachary F. Mainen (Champalimaud Research, 250+ publications, 2× ERC Advanced Awards) leads the group. Kyo Iigaya (Columbia University), whose published models of visual preference form the scientific core of the engine, is a core scientific partner. Daniel McNamee (Caltech PhD, publications in *Nature*, NeurIPS, ICML) leads the technical implementation. Stella Dong is pursuing her doctorate on the science of seeing beauty, developing the models that bridge low-level visual features and high-level meaning.

**The platform.** We build on HAAK, an open-source agentic platform for institutional intelligence. The art layer adds: a unified entity graph resolving 41 million entities across Iconclass (44K iconographic concepts), Getty AAT (2.5M art and architecture terms), artnet's 35-year market record, and Artsy's behavioral data. Feature extraction runs across visual, semantic, and market dimensions simultaneously. Taste profiles are computed locally — collector data stays on device by default and is never pooled for sale. The recommendation is grounded in the structure of the art world, not just its transaction history.

The moat is attribution. Every recommendation draws on a web of contributions: the artist's deposited works, the gallery's pricing history, the auction records going back decades, the platform's behavioral data. When a sale follows from a recommendation, the system can trace which of those contributions mattered and by how much — then distribute a portion of the value accordingly. An artist whose work informed the taste model that surfaced a $200K sale receives a measurable share. A gallery whose data shaped the collector's profile gets credit. This is not a philosophical commitment; it is a contractual one, enforced automatically when the sale clears. The flywheel runs in reverse from the current industry model: instead of data flowing up to platforms and value staying there, value flows back to the contributors. Artists who share data benefit when their data creates value. Platforms that build this way attract more and better data. Trust compounds.

**The proposal.** We are asking for three things: access to Artsy's behavioral dataset for a 90-day proof of concept; a technical counterpart from the Artsy data team; and feedback from a sample of collectors on whether the resulting recommendations feel right. In exchange: a working recommendation model within a week of data access, a full feature extraction pipeline within six months, and a transparent attribution architecture within twelve. The intellectual property is clear: HAAK is open source and forkable; the model trained on Artsy data belongs to this partnership. Competitors can use the platform. They cannot use what we build together.

The art market is not going to be the last domain this matters for. But it is the first one where the science, the data, and the right partners are in the room at the same time.
