HAAK and the Creative Economy
A vision for Beowolff
Zachary F. Mainen · Champalimaud Research · March 2026 · Confidential
From Recommendation to Intelligence

The recommendation engine is the first application. What it produces along the way is more valuable: a continuously updated, structurally rich model of how the art world is organised — which artists are connected to which institutions, how careers develop, where critical attention is moving, which collector cohorts are forming. This is not a database of facts; it is a living entity graph in which every relationship carries provenance, history, and epistemic weight. The agents that maintain it — HAAK's librarian and archivist roles — work continuously, ingesting new signals and updating the graph without human intervention. A gallery that feeds its data into this system does not just get recommendations back; it gets a mirror of its own position in the market, updated in real time.

Artist Discovery Before the Market

Price signals lag structural signals. By the time an artist's work is commanding attention at auction, the network has already shifted — new gallery relationships have formed, institutional exhibitions have accumulated, critical reception has changed. HAAK's entity graph captures these structural signals as they happen, not after they have been priced. An agent that monitors career trajectory in the graph can surface emerging artists at the moment their network position is changing, not after the market has caught up. This is the kind of intelligence that has historically been available only to the most connected insiders. Systematising it does not eliminate human judgment — it extends the range of the people who exercise it.

Provenance as Infrastructure

The art market's most persistent problems — fraud, unclear title, disputed attribution — are information problems. Provenance chains are recorded inconsistently, verified informally, and lost when institutions change hands. HAAK's ledger records every transaction it touches in a cryptographically auditable chain. An artwork that passes through the Beowolff system accumulates a provenance record that is immutable, timestamped, and queryable. This does not solve the problem of works that entered the market before the system existed — nothing does — but it creates a new standard for everything that comes after. Galleries and collectors who operate within the system have a provenance infrastructure that is qualitatively different from anything currently available.

Institutional Memory

Galleries lose knowledge when people leave. The relationships a senior director has built over twenty years — the collectors who trust her judgment, the artists whose careers she has shaped, the institutional history she carries — exist largely in her head. When she leaves, much of it goes with her. HAAK is designed to externalise this kind of knowledge: not as a contact database but as a structured record of relationships, decisions, and their outcomes, maintained by agents that do not retire. A gallery that runs on HAAK retains its intelligence across staff transitions. The institution persists even when the individuals do not.

The Operating System for the Creative Economy

The most ambitious version of what Beowolff can become is not a recommendation company. It is the governance layer for a new kind of creative market — one in which artists, galleries, collectors, and platforms have defined roles, clear authorities, and transparent value flows encoded in a constitutional architecture. Attribution — the mechanism by which data contributors are compensated when their data creates value — is the first instance of this. But the same architecture that distributes recommendation revenue can distribute any kind of value: exhibition fees, licensing income, secondary market royalties, research access. HAAK provides the infrastructure. Beowolff provides the domain knowledge, the relationships, and the first market to prove it works. The art market is not the last domain where this matters — but it is the right one to start with, because the stakes are high enough to demand it and small enough to move in.
