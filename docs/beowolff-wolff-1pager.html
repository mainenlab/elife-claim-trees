Why HAAK

Most recommendation engines treat data as fuel: collect it, process it, produce outputs, discard the provenance. HAAK is different in that it treats every piece of data — an artwork, a sale record, a collector interaction — as an entity with identity and history, connected to other entities through typed relations. An artist is not just an attribute of a work; they are an entity with a lineage, a gallery relationship, a critical reception trajectory, and a position in the market's evolving narrative. Building recommendation on top of this relational structure means the system understands not just what a collector has bought but what those choices mean in the context of the art world — which traditions they engage with, which market moments they respond to, which artists they encounter through which institutions. A system that knows this can recommend works the collector has not seen yet but would recognise immediately as right.

What It Enables

Because HAAK records the provenance of every contribution to the model, it can do something no other recommendation system does: trace which data drove which recommendation, and distribute value accordingly. When a sale follows from a recommendation, the system identifies which artists' deposited works, which galleries' pricing histories, and which auction records shaped the model that made the recommendation — and compensates them automatically, proportional to contribution. This is not a feature added on top of the recommendation engine; it is a consequence of how HAAK records information. The attribution architecture is the same architecture that makes the recommendation possible. They are the same system.

For Whom

Collectors get recommendations grounded in the actual structure of the art world, not in the aggregate behaviour of other buyers. Artists get measurable compensation when their data creates value for the platform — a reason to share more, and to share earlier. Galleries get visibility into which collectors are genuinely interested in their artists rather than which collectors have purchased works in a price range. Platforms get a recommendation engine that builds trust rather than extracting it. The art market currently has no infrastructure for this kind of structured value exchange. HAAK provides it.

Where It Goes

Taste is not domain-specific. A collector who responds to Rothko's engagement with scale and colour field also responds to certain music, certain architecture, certain film. HAAK's entity graph is designed to extend across domains — the same infrastructure that connects an artwork to its iconographic tradition connects a piece of music to its harmonic lineage. A recommendation that starts in art can eventually speak across the full range of what a person finds beautiful. Attribution follows the same extension: any creator whose work contributes to a cross-domain taste model participates in the value that model creates. This is the long-term proposition — not a better art recommendation engine, but the infrastructure through which the creative economy learns to compensate the people who make it possible.
