The renewal hypothesis is the paper's central interpretive bet: that the well-established behavioral phenomenon of context-dependent fear renewal (Bouton; Maren; Kalisch et al.) is grounded in PFC's capacity to encode contexts as separable representational entities. The hypothesis splits into two empirically testable parts. First, context coding should change with task demands: when reversal makes context-distinguishability behaviorally relevant (because the same cue can be safe in one context and dangerous in another), PFC should sharpen its context representations. Second, between-subject variability in this sharpening should track between-subject variability in renewal: subjects whose PFC encodes contexts more distinctly should show more reinstatement of the original acquisition memory in the original context. The hypothesis is not strong-form causal (PFC context coding is not shown to be necessary or sufficient for renewal here) — it is a representational-correlation hypothesis. The exploratory ROI analysis (acknowledged by the paper as exploratory) tests it directly.
