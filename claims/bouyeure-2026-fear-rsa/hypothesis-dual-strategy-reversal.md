This hypothesis sharpens a long-standing question in reversal learning — whether the brain "overwrites" prior associations or "interleaves" old and new — into a dissociation between two RSA measures (cue generalization vs item stability) and two anatomical territories (fear-network vs precuneus/IFG). It motivates the central empirical comparison: reversal should produce, simultaneously, increased between-item similarity for CS-+ in fear-network regions (generalization, mirroring acquisition) and increased within-item stability for CS+- in precuneus and IFG (item-specific traces preserving the changing cue's identity). The dissociation interpretation requires both effects to be real and located in different regions; if one fails, the dual-strategy account collapses into a single-strategy account. The corresponding interpretive synthesis claim (`dual-strategy-reversal-generalize-plus-specify`) reports the synthesis as established by the paper's results; this hypothesis claim is the pre-data prediction that motivates the experiment.
